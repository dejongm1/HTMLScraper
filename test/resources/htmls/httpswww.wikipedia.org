<p><b><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grace_Sherwood" title="Grace Sherwood">Grace Sherwood</a></b> (1660–1740), called the Witch of Pungo, is the last person known to have been convicted of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">witchcraft</a> in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Virginia</a>. A farmer, healer, and midwife, she was charged with witchcraft several times. In 1706, she was accused of bewitching Elizabeth Hill and causing her to <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">miscarry</a>. The court ordered that Sherwood's guilt or innocence be determined by <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_by_ordeal#Witch-hunts" title="Trial by ordeal">dunking</a> her in water: if she sank, she was innocent; if she did not, she was guilty. Sherwood floated to the surface, and may have spent up to eight years in jail. After being freed from prison and recovering her property from <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princess_Anne_County,_Virginia" title="Princess Anne County, Virginia">Princess Anne County</a>, she lived on her farm in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pungo,_Virginia" title="Pungo, Virginia">Pungo</a> from 1714 until her death at the age of about 80. On July 10, 2006, the 300th anniversary of Sherwood's conviction, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Governor_of_Virginia" title="Governor of Virginia">Governor</a> <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tim_Kaine" title="Tim Kaine">Tim Kaine</a> reversed the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscarriage_of_justice" title="Miscarriage of justice">miscarriage of justice</a> and restored her good name. A statue depicting her was erected in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virginia_Beach" class="mw-redirect" title="Virginia Beach">Virginia Beach</a>, close to the site of the colonial courthouse where she was tried. <p>The <b><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_London_Monopoly_locations" title="List of London Monopoly locations">locations on the standard British version of the board game <i>Monopoly</i></a></b> are set in London and were selected in 1935 by Victor Watson, managing director of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Waddington_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="John Waddington Limited">John Waddington Limited</a>. Watson became interested in the board game after his son Norman had tried the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parker_Brothers" title="Parker Brothers">Parker Brothers</a> original US version and recommended the company produce a board for the domestic market. He took his secretary Marjory Phillips on a day-trip from the head offices in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> to <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and the pair looked for suitable locations to use. The London version of the game was successful, and in 1936 it was exported to Continental Europe, Australia, New Zealand and South Africa, becoming the <i>de facto</i> standard board in the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth</a>. It became such a success in the UK that Waddingtons ran <i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a></i> competitions in locations depicted on the board <i>(pictured)</i>; one such contest was held on platforms 3 and 4 of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fenchurch_Street_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Fenchurch Street station">Fenchurch Street station</a>. The resulting board has been perennially popular around the world and the chosen locations have become familiar to millions.
